Subway – CRA user-centred design guide

CRA user-centred design guide

Subway

Subway navigation helps people accomplish a series of related tasks that are broken down into steps. It is ideal for displaying services and products.

On this page

  • When to use
  • What to avoid
  • Content and design
    • Understand the subway page types

    • Creating the index page
    • Creating step pages
    • Creating side-step pages, if needed
  • Research and blog posts

When to use

  • To break up long and complex content over a small group of pages
  • To provide persistent navigation that follows the user journey through a specific service or product

What to avoid

  • Do not use for any reason except to guide the user through a series of related tasks
  • Do not misuse the patterns, as this:
    • Creates inconsistency from one subway product to another across canada.ca
    • Risks breaking the following principles:
      • Information architecture principles InfoZone
      • Visual design principles InfoZone
      • Interaction design principles InfoZone

Content and design

The subway is group of different page patterns. Each pattern has its own rules and design specifications.

To create proper subway pages:

  1. Understand the subway page types

    There are 3 page types within the subway pattern:

    • Index page (required)
    • Step page (required)
    • Side-step page (optional)

    This creates a minimum of 2 levels of information architecture (IA) that can grow to support 3 levels of IA, if needed:

    • First level (mandatory):
      Index page

      Screenshot: Index page

    • Second level (mandatory):
      Step pages

      Screenshot: Step page

    • Third level (optional):
      Side-step pages

      If a section of content is too complex or varied to effectively fit on a single step page, you can create a sub-level of IA under a specific step for that content.

      There are 2 options:

      • Non-connected page

        Screenshot: Non-connected side-step page

      • Connected page

        Screenshot: Connected side-step page

    These patterns create the IA framework for the content. Details about each pattern type are provided below.

  2. Determine the structure and flow of content

    The flow of content across the various subway pages creates a "happy path" for the user to view the content in a logical progression. It starts with the basic details and then move the user through the detailed process. The "happy path" flow:

    1. Introduce the concept

      Create a small introductory statement that explains what the service and product is.

      • If its easy and brief to describe

        Typically the introductory statement fits on the index page.

        It is a short paragraph or 2, and is the first thing to appear on the index page, above the subway navigation.

        For example:

        • Canada Dental Benefit
        • Canada Workers Benefit
      • If it's complicated to describe

        Sometimes the concept is too complex to reasonably describe on the index page. When this happens, feature basic details on the index page and use the first step page in the subway to provide more detail.

        For example:

        • Index page the basic details:
          Disability tax credit (DTC)
          • First step page has more detail:
            What is the DTC
        • Index page the basic details:
          Scientific Research and Experimental Development (SR&ED) tax incentives
          • First step page has more detail:
            What are SR&ED tax incentives

    2. Explain who it's for

      This is often where eligibility information goes.

      Common link text to consider in for the subway navigation:

      • Who can apply
      • Who can claim
      • Who can file
      • Who is eligible
        "Who is eligible" should rarely be used

        Use the plain language terms "apply" or "claim" instead of "eligble". However, in rare situations, "eligible" may be used if the other terms might cause user confusion.

      • How much you can get
      • Determine if you need to register
      • Periods you can apply for
      • Expenses you can claim
      • Which of your employees qualify

    3. Explain what core activity the user has to do

      This where the core activity goes. The core activitiy typically deals with calculating, applying, claiming, registering, filing and submitting.

      Common link text to consider in for the subway navigation:

      • Get ready to apply
      • Get ready to claim
      • Get ready to file
      • When to apply
      • When to claim
      • When to file
      • Calculate your [x]
      • How to apply
      • How to claim
      • How to file

    4. Wrap-up with any follow-up activities

      This where the activities go that happen after the core activity has been done.

      Common link text to consider in for the subway navigation:

      • Validating your application
      • Our review and decision
      • After you apply
      • After you claim
      • After you file
      • When and how to get your payment
      • Impact on your taxes
      • Change or cancel your claim
      • Return a payment
      • Keep getting your payments

    5. Provide contact details (optional)

      If there are specific phone numbers or mailing addresses associated, it is optional to create a contact page.

      The common navigation link is:

      • Contact us

    6. Ensure the subway navigation is persistent

      The subway menu is a persistent design. You must consider this when planning your subway IA, as the menu must follow the users throughout the entire process.

      This means:

      • Links that take the user away from the subway pattern cannot be featured in the subway menu. Instead, those links should be in the content area of a page, at the point-of-need.
      • If you are linking across different page owners or domains (for example, the content pages are on canada.ca but the calculator is on an application server), you must still carry across the subway menu if you cross-link within the subway menu.

      This persistent navigation ensures that no subway link takes the user to a page that detaches them from the "happy path".

  3. Creating the index page

    This is the main landing page for the product or service. Use this page to introduce step pages.

    Screenshot: Index page

    h1 heading

    The h1 heading on the index page is the main heading for the service or product. The heading you choose will automatically be repurposed as the page's title, which is an invisible tag that search engines use for their search results.

    In order to have quality search engine results, the h1 on the index page must be 100% unique within the entire Government of Canada. A unique h1 ensures that a search engine will display meaningful results, and not show links that seem to compete with or overlap each other.

    If your heading is vague, it risks user confusion as other pages on canada.ca may have similar headings, causing those pages to also appear on the user's search results.

    For clarity, make the heading unique enough that it doesn't overlap or duplicate any current or future page another department or agency creates.

    Typically the subway pattern is applied to a product (a noun). In those cases, there is less chance of other Government of Canada pages showing up in search results and competing with your content.

    For example:

    • Canada Dental Benefit
    • Canada Workers Benefit

    However, when a subway's index page is designed around a task (a verb), the heading can often be too vague because a task can be common across several departments and agencies. In this situation, be sure to make your heading specific.

    For example:

    • Vague index page h1: Register for an account
    • Unique index page h1: Register for a payroll account
    Breadcrumb
    • The last link in the breadcrumb path must be the IA parent page of this index page
    • The parent page must also feature a link back to the subway index page, to ensure the index page is not orphaned
    Content area
    • Must include a short description of the service or product directly below the h1
    • Must include a subway navigation that includes both the:
      • Link to the step page
      • A short description (doormat) of what that step page is about
    • May include supportive links to products or pages outside the subway.
    Subway navigation
    • Must be displayed in the main content area
    • Must feature the links to the subway step pages
      • Links should be as concise as possible, as the double h1 design that appears on the subsequent step pages carries over the name of the service or product. For example:
        • Do: How to apply
        • Don't: How to apply for the Disability Tax Credit (DTC)
    • Must have a reasonable number of links (aim to have no more than 8)
    • Must only link to the first level of IA
    • Must link to pages that have the corresponding subway menu, as no link can take the user outside the subway menu
    • Must describe each link, using the doormat link description rules
    Supportive links
    • These links:
      • Should be avoided whenever possible, as it can distract a user from their task
      • Apply to the entire service or process
      • Appear below the subway menu, under its own heading called "Related"
        • If there are several supportive links and they can be bundled together into groups, you can create multiple specific headings to place the supportive links under instead of using the singlar "Related" heading
    Pagination buttons
    • Backwards button:
      • None, since this is the root page for this product or service
    • Forwards button:
      • None, since the user should make a choice from the subway menu that is promintently featured in the content area
    title tag

    Format: <title>[Service or product name] - Canada.ca</title>

  4. Creating step pages

    These are the main step pages. Use this pattern on each page that is part of the first level of IA within the subway navigation.

    Screenshot: Step page

    h1 headingsh1 headings
    • First h1 heading: This is the smaller dark grey heading that correspondes with the navigation, and must match the h1 on the index page

    • Second h1 heading: This is the larger black heading that:

      • Focuses on a specific task for that page
      • Typically does not repeat the product or service name already mentioned in the first h1

    The concept of having 2 h1s is documented in Service Canada's: Design decision 12 - Double H1 on a single page

    Breadcrumb
    The subway's index page must be the parent page in the breadcrumb
    Content area

    Everthing in the content area must adhere to:

    • Writing rules
    • Design system components for styling

    When there is a table of contents on the page, consider placing a simple page summary immediately below the second h1 heading. This can help a search engine algorigthm find a good page description to display in the search results. It is not a guarentee that the search results page will use your page summary, but it helps increase the chances.

    Subway navigation
    • Has a header called "Sections"
    • Has a vertical line that connects the step pages together
    • Has a solid fill circle on the active page
    • Has plain language, task-based content that helps the user to complete the step
    • Links to pages that have the assoicated subway menu (you can't link to pages outside the subway menu from a subway link)
    Supportive links
    Appear within the content area at the point of need
    Pagination buttons
    • Backwards button:
      • Do not use a previous button as you want to encourage users to move forward
    • Forwards button:
      • Position on the bottom right of the content
      • Include a right arrow icon after the link text
      • Link to the next step page (if this is the last step then do not include any forward pagination)
    title tag
    Format: <title>[Step page] - [Service or product name] - Canada.ca</title>

  5. Creating side-step pages, if needed

    Side-step pages are an optional element that can be added when you need to break up a large step into smaller steps or to outline a side journey for specific audiences related to the main step.

    There are 2 types of side-steps:

    • Non-connected side-steps

      Use this navigation for when side-steps are not part of a sequential flow

    • Connected side-steps

      Use this navigation for when side-steps are part of a sequential flow

    1. Determine how the step page will link to the side-step page

      If you create a step page that has side-steps, then the content area design will change, as the primarily focus of that content area is to now feature the navigaiton to the side-steps.

      There are 3 ways to display the navigation on the step page:

      • As tiles

        Use when there is no sequential order to the side-steps:

        • Use the grid system to create link tiles that also include doormat link description text
        • Do not include any pagination buttons, as the user should make a choice from the navigation links that are promintently featured in content area
        • Sample:

          [Link 1]

          Short description

          [Link 2]

          Short description

          [Link 3]

          Short description

          [Link 4]

          Short description

          View code 
          adsfdas
          Live example: Who can claim - Home office expenses for employees
      • As a numbered list

        Use to create a step-by-step sub-process for content that is best viewed in a sequential order:

        • Introduce with the heading "Steps"
        • Use stylized steps that include doormat link description text
        • Do not include any pagination buttons, as the user should make a choice from the navigation links that are promintently featured in content area
        • Sample:

          Steps

          1. [First task]

            Short description

          2. [Second task]

            Short description

          3. [Third task]

            Short description

          4. [Fourth task]

            Short description

          View code 
          adsfdas
          Live example: How to prepare your claim - Scientific Research and Experimental Development (SR&ED) tax incentives
      • Subtle navigation links

        Use on rare occasions when:

        • The user can likely get what they need from the step page content section
        • The side-step only contains supportive or secondary content

        In this situation, populate the step page content as normal, and then feature the links to the side-steps at the point-of-need in that content.

      • Sample:

        Content here

        Content here

        [Leading statement], refer to: [Side-step link]

        Content here

        • Step page live example: Calculate your expenses - Home office expenses for employees
          • Side-step page live example: How the claim is calculated - Home office expenses for employees

    2. Design the side-step pages

      The side-step menu only appears when the user is within that sub-level of the IA. This supports the IA's principle of disclosure InfoZone.

      Screenshot: Non-connected page

      Screenshot: Connected page

      h1 headings
      The steps pages in a subway have 2 first-level (h1) headings:
      • First h1 heading: This is the smaller dark grey heading and must match the h1 on the index page
      • Second h1 heading: This is the larger black heading and...

      This is discussed in Service Canada's: Design decision 12 - Double H1 on a single page

      Breadcrumb
      The breadcrumb of these pages should link to both the main index page and the parent step page that contains the side-steps (e.g. Index page > Step page > side-step page).
      Content area

      Everthing in the content area must adhere to:

      • Writing rules
      • Design system components for styling

      When there is a table of contents on the page, consider placing a simple page summary immediately below the main h1 heading. This can help a search engine algorigthm correctly describe the page in the search results.

      Subway navigation
      • Has a header called "Sections"
      • Has a vertical line that connects the step pages together
      • Has a solid fill circle on the active page
      • Has plain language, task-based content that helps the user to complete the step
      • Links to pages that have the assoicated subway menu (you can't link to pages outside the subway menu from a subway link)
      Supportive links
      Appear within the content area at the point of need
      Pagination buttons
      • On non-connected side-step pages:
        • Backwards button:
          • Position on the bottom left of the content
          • Include a left arrow icon before the link text
          • Link back to the parent step page
        • Forwards button:
          • Position on the bottom right of the content
          • Include a right arrow icon after the link text
          • Link to the next natural step the user will take (often this is a level up to the next step page)
      • On connected side-step pages:
        • Backwards button: Do not use a previous button as you want to encourage users to move forward
        • Forwards button:
          • Position on the bottom right of the content
          • Include a right arrow icon after the link text
          • Link to the next side-step page, or if this is the last side-step, then level up to the next step page
      title tag
      Format: <title>[Side-step page] - [Service or product name] - Canada.ca</title>

Research and blog posts

This pattern:

  • Was developed with CRA during an optimization project on the Canada Child Benefit, which confirmed it:
    • Works well on mobile devices
    • Is an effective way to show the relationship between related pages
  • Has been further tested on numerous other benefit programs, including the COVID-19 benefits and subsidies
  • Uses 2 h1 headings on the step pages and the side-step pages. This is discussed in Service Canada's: Design decision 12 - Double H1 on a single page
